    Section 1.1 The Problem of Area

    One of the oldest problems in mathematics is computing the area of planar regions. For example, the Rhind mathematical papyrus
     1 
    en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhind_Mathematical_Papyrus
    (16th century BCE) makes clear that the ancient Egyptians knew how to compute the areas of rectangles, triangles, and even discs (using the approximation \(\pi \approx \frac{256}{81}\)).
    Despite enormous progress in computing the areas and volumes of geometric figures, the area of a parabolic segment (a planar region bounded by a parabola and a line) eluded ancient mathematicians until the 3rd century BCE, when the Greek mathematician Archimedes
     2 
    en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes
    successfully computed the area of a parabolic segment
     3 
    en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrature_of_the_Parabola
    using the method of exhaustion
     4 
    en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_exhaustion
    , an ancient precursor to calculus independently developed in both ancient Greece (5th century BCE) and ancient China (3rd century CE).
    In this section, we’ll use the method of exhaustion to carry out an area computation closely related to Archimedes’ computation; along the way, you’ll encounter your first example of proof by contradiction
     5 
    gvsuoer.github.io/sundstrom-textbook/S_contradiction.html
    . Thus, we’ll prove the following claim.

    Theorem 1.1.1 (after Archimedes).

    Let \(U\) denote the planar region bounded by the parabola \(y=x^2\) and the lines \(y=0\) and \(x=1\text{,}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} U \defn \set{(x,y) \in \R^2}{0 \leq x \leq 1,\, 0 \leq y \leq x^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Then \(\Area(U) = \frac{1}{3}\text{.}\)

    Note 1.1.3.

    Let’s just take a moment to review some of the notation and terminology we’ve just used.
    • A planar region is a subset of \(\R^2\text{.}\)
    • The notation \(p \in A\) means “\(p\) is an element of the set \(A\text{.}\)”
    • The notation \(\set{p \in A}{Q(p)}\) means “the set of all elements \(p\) of the set \(A\) such that the condition \(Q(p)\) is true.” For example, \(\set{x \in \R}{x \gt 0}\) is the set of all elements \(x\) of the set \(\R\) such that \(x \gt 0\text{,}\) i.e., the set of all positive real numbers.
    • The notation \(x \defn y\) means “\(x\) is defined to be \(y\text{.}\)” This notation isn’t yet completely standard in mathematics, but we’ll use it throughout this course.
    Before continuing, let me be precise about the properties of area we’ll use.

    Assumption 1.1.4.

    One can give a formal definition
     6 
    en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#Formal_definition
    of the area of planar regions, which is realized by a construction called Jordan content. We’ll assume that all planar regions under consideration have a well-defined area. We’ll also assume without proof the following basic facts about area.
    1. If \(R\) is a rectangle with base \(b\) and height \(h\text{,}\) then \(\Area(R) = bh\text{.}\) In particular, a line segment (rectangle with base or height equal to \(0\)) has area equal to \(0\text{.}\)
    2. If \(A\) is empty, then \(\Area(A) = 0\text{.}\)
    3. Let \(A_1,\dotsc,A_N\) be planar regions with well-defined area. Suppose that \(\Area(A_i \cap A_j) = 0\) for all \(1 \leq i,j \leq N\) satisfying \(i \neq j\text{.}\) Then
      \begin{equation*} \Area(A_1 \cup \cdots \cup A_N) = \Area(A_1) + \cdots + \Area(A_N)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    4. Let \(A\) and \(B\) be planar regions with well-defined area. If \(A \subseteq B\text{,}\) then \(\Area(A) \leq \Area(B)\text{.}\)

    Note 1.1.5.

    Again, let’s review some of the notation we’ve just used.
    • Let \(A\) and \(B\) be subsets of a set \(X\text{.}\)
      • The union of \(A\) and \(B\) is the set of all elements of \(X\) that lie in \(A\) or \(B\text{,}\) which we denote by \(A \cup B\text{.}\)
      • The intersection of \(A\) and \(B\) is the set of all elements of \(X\) that lie in both \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) which we denote by \(A \cap B\text{.}\)
    • Let \(A\) and \(B\) be sets. The notation \(A \subset B\) means “\(A\) is a subset of \(B\text{,}\)” equivalently, “\(A\) is contained in \(B\text{.}\)”
    Our first step is to show how to decompose \(\Area(U)\) into the sum of areas of smaller vertical slices of equal width.

    Lemma 1.1.7.

    Let \(N\) be a natural number. For each \(0 \leq k \leq N-1\text{,}\) let
    \begin{equation*} U_{k;N} \defn \set{(x,y) \in U}{\frac{k}{N} \leq x \leq \frac{k+1}{N}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Then \(\Area(U) = \Area(U_{0;N}) + \Area(U_{1;N}) + \cdots + \Area(U_{N-1;N})\text{.}\)

    Convention 1.1.8.

    In this course, a natural number is a positive integer. Thus, the set of all natural numbers is \(\N = \{1,2,3,\dotsc\}\text{.}\)

    Proof of Lemma 1.1.7.

    I’ll be a little sketchy with this proof since it concerns the underlying geometry of this problem, which is intuitively clear but finnicky to write out in complete detail.
    Let us first show that \(U = U_{0;N} \cup \cdots \cup U_{N-1;N}\text{.}\) On the one hand, each \(U_{k;N}\) is contained in \(U\) by construction, so that \(U_{0;N} \cup \cdots \cup U_{N-1;N} \subset U\text{.}\) On the other hand, since \(0 \lt \frac{1}{N} \lt \frac{2}{N} \lt \cdots \lt \frac{N-1}{N} \lt 1 \text{,}\) every point of \(U\) is contained in some \(U_{k;N}\text{,}\) and hence in \(U_{0;N} \cup \cdots \cup U_{N-1;N} \subset U\text{,}\) so that \(U \subset U_{0;N} \cup \cdots \cup U_{N-1;N}\text{.}\)
    Next, let us show that \(\Area(U_{i;N} \cap U_{j;N}) = 0\) for all \(0 \leq i,j \leq N-1\) such that \(i \neq j\text{.}\) Let \(i\) and \(j\) be given. If \(i\) and \(j\) are consective integers, say, \(j = i+1\text{,}\) then \(U_{i;N} \cap U_{j;N}\) is the vertical line segment \(\set{(x,y) \in \R^2}{x=i+1,\,y=0}\text{,}\) so that \(\Area(U_{i;N} \cap U_{j;N}) = 0\) by Item 1. Otherwise, \(U_{i;N}\) and \(U_{j;N}\) are disjoint, i.e. \(U_{i;N} \cap U_{j;N}\) is empty, so that \(\Area(U_{i;N} \cap U_{j;N}) = 0\) by Item 2.
    At last, we have shown that \(U = U_{0;N} \cup \cdots \cup U_{N-1;N}\text{,}\) where \(\Area(U_{i;N} \cap U_{j;N}) = 0\) whenever \(i \neq j\text{.}\) Thus, by Item 3, it follows that \(\Area(U) = \Area(U_{0;N}) + \cdots + \Area(U_{N-1;N})\text{.}\)
    Our next step is to estimate the area of each vertical slice.

    Lemma 1.1.9.

    Let \(N\) be a natural number, let \(0 \leq k \leq N-1\text{,}\) and let \(U_{k;N}\) be defined as in Lemma 1.1.7. Then \(\frac{k^2}{N^3} \leq \Area(U_{k;N}) \leq \frac{(k+1)^2}{N^3}\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Again, I’ll be a little sketchy with this proof since it concerns the underlying geometry of this problem, which is intuitively clear but a little finnicky to write out in complete detail.
    Recall that \(a^2 \leq b^2\) whenever \(0 \leq a \leq b\text{.}\) Thus, it follows that \(\frac{k^2}{N^2} \leq x^2 \leq \frac{(k+1)^2}{N^2}\) whenever \(\frac{k}{N} \leq x \leq \frac{k+1}{N}\text{.}\) Hence, it follows that \(U_{k;N}\) contains the smaller rectangle
    \begin{equation*} R_1 \defn \set{(x,y) \in \R^2}{\frac{k}{N} \leq x \leq \frac{k+1}{N}, \, 0 \leq y \leq \frac{k^2}{N^2}} \end{equation*}
    and is contained by the larger rectangle
    \begin{equation*} R_2 \defn \set{(x,y) \in \R^2}{\frac{k}{N} \leq x \leq \frac{k+1}{N}, \, 0 \leq y \leq \frac{(k+1)^2}{N^2}}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so that \(\Area(R_1) \leq \Area(U_{k;N}) \leq \Area(R_2)\) by Item 4.
    On the one hand, since \(R_1\) is a rectangle with base \(\frac{1}{N}\) and height \(\frac{k^2}{N^2}\text{,}\) it follows that \(\Area(R_1) = \frac{k^2}{N^3}\) by Item 1. On the other hand, since \(R_2\) is a rectangle with base \(\frac{1}{N}\) and height \(\frac{(k+1)^2}{N^2}\text{,}\) it follows that \(\Area(R_2) =\frac{(k+1)^2}{N^3}\) by Item 1. Thus, at last, it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{k^2}{N^3} = \Area(R_1) \leq \Area(U_{k;N}) \leq \Area(R_2) = \frac{(k+1)^2}{N^3}. \end{equation*}
    Our next step is to sum up our upper and lower estimates on the areas of the vertical slices to obtain upper and lower estimates for \(\Area(U)\text{.}\)

    Lemma 1.1.11.

    For every natural number \(N\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{N^3}(1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots (N-1)^2) \leq \Area(U) \leq \frac{1}{N^3}(1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots N^2)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Let \(M\) be a natural number. On the one hand, \(\Area(U) = \Area(U_{0;N}) + \cdots + \Area(U_{k;N})\) by Lemma 1.1.7. On the other hand, \(\frac{k^2}{N^3} \leq \Area(U_{k;N}) \leq \frac{(k+1)^2}{N^3}\) for each \(0 \leq k \leq N-1\) by Lemma 1.1.9. Thus, we conclude that
    \begin{align*} \Area(U) \amp = \Area(U_{0,1/N}) + \Area(U_{1/N,2/N}) + \cdots + \Area(U_{(N-1)/N,1})\\ \amp \geq \frac{0^2}{N} + \frac{1^2}{N} + \cdots \frac{(N-1)^2}{N} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{N}\left(1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots + (N-1)^2\right) \end{align*}
    and
    \begin{align*} \Area(U) \amp = \Area(U_{0,1/N}) + \Area(U_{1/N,2/N}) + \cdots + \Area(U_{(N-1)/N,1}) \\ \amp \leq \frac{1^2}{N} + \frac{2^2}{N} + \cdots \frac{N^2}{N}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{N}\left(1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots + N^2\right) \text{.} \end{align*}
    Note that our upper and lower estimates for \(\Area(U)\) aren’t yet in a particularly useful form. We’ll need the following familiar fact (also due to Archimedes!) to simplify our estimates.

    Theorem 1.1.12 (Archimedes).

    For every natural number \(k\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} 1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots + k^2 = \frac{1}{6}k(k+1)(2k+1). \end{equation*}
    We can now simplify our upper and lower estimates for \(\Area(U)\) into a usable form that makes clear why we should expect \(\Area(U) = \frac{1}{3}\text{.}\)

    Lemma 1.1.13.

    For every natural number \(N\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{3}-\frac{1}{2N} + \frac{1}{6N^2} \leq \Area(U) \leq \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2N} + \frac{1}{6N^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    By Lemma 1.1.11 and Theorem 1.1.12,
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{N^3}(1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots N^2) \amp = \frac{1}{N^3} \cdot \frac{1}{6}N(N+1)(2N+1) \\ \amp = \frac{1}{6}\left(1+\frac{1}{N}\right)\left(2+\frac{1}{N}\right) \\ \amp = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2N} + \frac{1}{6N^2} \text{,} \end{align*}
    so that, in turn,
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{N^3}(1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots (N-1)^2) \amp = \frac{1}{N^3}(1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots + N^2) - \frac{1}{N^3}N^2 \\ \amp = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2N} + \frac{1}{6N^2} - \frac{1}{N} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{2N} + \frac{1}{6N^2} \text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus,
    \begin{equation*} \Area(U) \leq \frac{1}{N^3}(1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots N^2) = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2N} + \frac{1}{6N^2} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \Area(U) \geq \frac{1}{N^3}(1^2 + 2^2 + \cdots (N-1)^2) = \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{2N} + \frac{1}{6N^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We’re almost ready for our final step, where we show that the assumption \(\Area(U) \neq \frac{1}{3}\) gives rise to a contradiction. The heart of our argument will be that the remainders in our upper and lower estimates for \(\Area(U)\) can be made as small as we like by making \(N\) sufficiently large. This will reduce to the intuitively obvious fact that \(\frac{1}{N}\) can be made as small as well like by making \(N\) sufficiently large, but this is actually a profound fact about numbers that is commonly attributed to Archimedes but was credited by Archimedes himself to Eudoxus of Cnidus
     7 
    en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus
    .

    Axiom 1.1.14. Archimedean property of the real numbers.

    For every positive real number \(\epsilon\text{,}\) there exists a natural number \(N\) such that
    \begin{equation*} 0 \lt \frac{1}{N} \lt \epsilon\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Armed with the Archimedean property of the real numbers, we can now complete our proof.

    Proof of Theorem 1.1.1.

    We’ll use a proof by contradiction. Thus, assume the negation of our claim, i.e., \(\Area(U) \neq \frac{1}{3}\text{;}\) we’ll use this assumption to prove something that is false.
    Let \(\Delta \defn \Area(U) - \frac{1}{3}\text{.}\) Since we assumed that \(\Area(U) \neq \frac{1}{3}\text{,}\) it follows that \(\Delta \neq 0\text{,}\) and hence that \(\abs{\Delta} \gt 0\text{.}\) Thus, by Axiom 1.1.14, there exists a natural number \(N\) such that \(0 \lt \frac{1}{N} \lt \abs{\Delta}\text{.}\) Hence, by Lemma 1.1.13,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{3}-\frac{1}{2N} + \frac{1}{6N^2} \leq \Area(U) \leq \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2N} + \frac{1}{6N^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Now, since \(\Delta \neq 0\text{,}\) either \(\Delta \gt 0\) or \(\Delta \lt 0\text{.}\) Let us consider each case in turn.
    1. Suppose that \(\Delta \gt 0\text{,}\) so that \(\abs{\Delta} = \Delta\text{.}\) Then
      \begin{align*} \Delta \amp \leq \left(\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2N} + \frac{1}{6N^2}\right) - \frac{1}{3} \\ \amp = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{N} + \frac{1}{6} \cdot \frac{1}{N} \cdot \frac{1}{N} \\ \amp \lt \frac{1}{2} \cdot \epsilon + \frac{1}{2} \cdot \abs{\Delta} \cdot 1 \\ \amp = \abs{\Delta} \\ \amp = \Delta \text{,} \end{align*}
      so that \(\Delta \lt \Delta\text{,}\) which is false.
    2. Suppose that \(\Delta \lt 0\text{,}\) so that \(\abs{\Delta} = -\Delta\text{.}\) Then
      \begin{align*} \Delta \amp \geq \left(\frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{2N} + \frac{1}{6N^2}\right) - \frac{1}{3} \\ \amp \geq -\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{N} \\ \amp \gt -\frac{1}{2} \cdot \epsilon \\ \amp \gt -\abs{\Delta} \\ \amp = \Delta \text{,} \end{align*}
      so that \(\Delta \gt \Delta\text{,}\) which is false.
    Thus, either way, the assumption that \(\Area(A) \neq \frac{1}{3}\) implies something false.
