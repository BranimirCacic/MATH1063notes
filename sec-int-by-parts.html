    Section 2.2 Integration by Parts

    In Section 2.1, we saw how the chain rule for the derivative of a composition yielded a technique of integration called integration by substitution. In this section, we’ll see how the Leibniz rule for the derivative of a product yields a technique of integration called integration by parts. Our account is complementary to Section 6.2
     1 
    opentext.uleth.ca/apex-calculus/sec_IBP.html
    of APEX Calculus and Section 7.1 of Strang’s Calculus.

    Theorem 2.2.1.

    Let \(u\) and \(v\) be continuously differentiable on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b u(x)v^\prime(x)\du{x} = \eval{u(x)v(x)}{a}{b} - \int_a^b u^\prime(x)v(x)\du{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    By the Leibniz rule for differentiation,
    \begin{equation*} (u \cdot v)^\prime = u^\prime \cdot v + u \cdot v^\prime\text{,} \end{equation*}
    On the one hand, by Theorem 1.7.3,
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b (u \cdot v)^\prime = (u \cdot v)(b) - (u \cdot v)(a) = f(b)g(b) - f(a)g(a)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    On the other hand, since \(f\text{,}\) \(f^\prime\text{,}\) \(g\text{,}\) and \(g^\prime\) are all continuous on \([a,b]\text{,}\) so too are \(u^\prime \cdot v\) and \(u \cdot v^\prime\text{,}\) so that \(u^\prime \cdot v\) and \(u \cdot v^\prime\) are both integrable on \([a,b]\) by Theorem 1.6.6, and hence, by Proposition 1.4.11,
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b (u \cdot v)^\prime = \int_a^b (u^\prime \cdot v + u \cdot v^\prime) = \int_a^b u^\prime \cdot v + \int_a^b u \cdot v^\prime\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus, at last,
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b u^\prime \cdot v + \int_a^b u \cdot v^\prime = u(b)v(b) - u(a)v(a)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which we can solve for \(\int_a^b u \cdot v^\prime\) to get
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b u \cdot v^\prime = \left(u(b)v(b) - u(a)v(a)\right) - \int_a^b u^\prime \cdot v\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Rewriting this equality in Leibniz notation gives the claim as stated.
    In practice, integration by parts is often carried out at the level of indefinite integration. Thus, suppose that \(u\) and \(v\) are continuously differentiable on some interval \(I\text{,}\) so that \(u^\prime \cdot v\) and \(u \cdot v^\prime\) are both continuous on \(I\) and hence have antiderivatives by the first fundamental theorem of calculus (Theorem 1.6.4). Then
    \begin{equation*} (u \cdot v)^\prime = u^\prime \cdot v + u \cdot v^\prime\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which we can solve for \(u \cdot v^\prime\) to get
    \begin{equation*} u \cdot v^\prime = (u \cdot v)^\prime - u^\prime \cdot v\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(u \cdot v\) is an antiderivative of \((u \cdot v)^\prime\text{,}\) it therefore follows that
    \begin{equation*} \int u(x)v^\prime(x)\du{x} = u(x)v(x) - \int u^\prime(x)v(x)\du{x} \end{equation*}
    which one traditionally writes as
    \begin{equation} \int u \du{v} = uv - \int v \du{u}\text{,}\tag{2.2.1} \end{equation}
    where \(\du{u} \defn u^\prime(x)\du{x}\) and \(\du{v} \defn v^\prime(x)\du{x}\text{.}\)

    Heuristic 2.2.2. Integration by parts in practice.

    To compute \(\int_a^b h(x) \du{x}\) using integration by parts:
    1. Choose a function \(u\) such that \(h(x)\du{x}\) obviously factorizes as
      \begin{equation*} h(x)\du{x} = u\du{v}\text{,} \end{equation*}
      where \(\du{v} \defn v^\prime(x)\du{x}\) for \(v\) an unknown function with chosen derivative \(v^\prime(x)\text{.}\) You want to choose \(u\) (explicitly) and\(v\) (implicitly) in such a way that \(u^\prime\) is much simpler than \(u\) and \(v\) can’t be much more complicated than \(v^\prime\text{.}\)
    2. Reverse engineer \(v\) via \(\int \du{v} = v(x) + C\text{.}\)
    3. Using (2.2.1), write
      \begin{equation*} \int u \du{v} = uv - \int v \du{u}\text{,} \end{equation*}
      and compute \(\int v \du{u}\) by any means necessary. If you’ve chosen \(u\) and \(v\) correctly, the new indefinite integral \(\int v \du{u}\) will be easier than \(\int u \du{v}\text{.}\)
    4. Apply the second fundamental theorem of calculus (Theorem 1.7.3) to compute \(\int_a^b h(x)\du{x}\) in terms of \(\int h(x)\du{x} = \int u \du{v}\text{.}\)
    Moreover, note that the first three steps yield a method for computing the indefinite integral \(\int h(x) \du{x}\text{.}\)

    Warning 2.2.3.

    The traditional use of \(u\) and \(v\) in integration by parts has nothing to do with the traditional use of \(u\) in integration by substitution!

    Example 2.2.4.

    Let’s apply integration by parts to compute \(\int x\eu^x \du{x}\text{.}\)
    1. Choose \(u(x) \defn x\text{,}\) so that \(x\eu^x \du{x} = u(x) \eu^x \du{x}\text{,}\) and hence \(\du{v} = \eu^x \du{x}\text{.}\)
    2. Since \(\int\du{v} = \int \eu^x \du{x} = \eu^x + C\text{,}\) take \(v(x) \defn \eu^x\text{.}\) Note that \(u^\prime(x) = 1\) is much simpler than \(u(x) = x\text{,}\) while \(v(x) = \eu^x\) is no more complicated than \(v^\prime(x) = \eu^x\text{.}\)
    3. Since \(\du{u} = u^\prime(x)\du{x} = 1\du{x} = \du{x}\text{,}\) we get
      \begin{align*} \int x\eu^x \du{x} \amp = \int u \du{v}\\ \amp = uv - \int v \du{u}\\ \amp = x\eu^x - \int \eu^x \du{x} \\ \amp = x\eu^x - \eu^x + C \end{align*}
    You may be wondering how you should choose \(u\) and \(\du{v}\text{.}\) APEX Calculus

    provides the following guidance.

    Heuristic 2.2.5. ‘LIATE’.

    Consider the following informal hierarchy of elementary functions:
    1. Logarithmic functions;
    2. Inverse trigonometric functions;
    3. Algebraic functions (e.g., polynomials, rational functions, \(n\)th roots);
    4. Trigonometric functions;
    5. Exponential.
    Suppose that \(h = p \cdot q\text{,}\) where \(p\) ranks higher on the list than \(q\text{.}\) Then write \(\int h(x) \du{x} = \int u \du{v}\text{,}\) where \(u(x) \defn p(x)\) and \(\du{v} = q(x)\du{x}\text{.}\)

    Example 2.2.6.

    Let’s compute \(\int x \sin(x)\du{x}\text{.}\)
    1. Note that \(x\) is Algebraic while \(\sin(x)\) is Trigonometric. Since A precedes T in LIATE, take \(u(x) \defn x\) and \(\du{v} = \sin(x)\du{x}\text{.}\)
    2. Since \(\int\du{v} = \int\sin(x)\du{x} = -\cos(x)+C\text{,}\) take \(v(x) \defn -\cos(x)\text{.}\)
    3. Since \(\du{u} = 1 \du{x} = \du{x}\text{,}\) compute
      \begin{align*} \int x \sin(x)\du{x} \amp = \int u\du{v} \\ \amp = uv - \int v\du{u} \\ \amp = x(-\cos(x)) - \int (-\cos(x))\du{x}\\ \amp = -x\cos(x) + \int \cos(x) \du{x}\\ \amp = -x\cos(x) + \sin(x) + C\text{.} \end{align*}
    Suppose you want to compute \(\int h(x) \du{x}\text{,}\) where \(h\) is logarithmic or inverse trigonometric. Note that \(h = h \cdot 1\text{,}\) where the constant \(1\) is Algebraic and hence outranked by \(h\text{.}\) Thus, LIATE suggests taking \(u \defn h\) and \(\du{v} = 1\du{x}\text{,}\) which yields the obvious choice of \(v(x) \defn x\text{.}\) The idea is that \(h^\prime\) will be so much simpler in form than \(h\) that \(\int v\du{u} = \int x u^\prime(x)\du{x}\) will still be simpler to compute than \(\int h(x)\du{x} = \int u \du{v}\text{.}\)

    Example 2.2.7.

    Let’s compute \(\int \log(x) \du{x}\text{.}\)
    1. Since \(\log\) is Logarithmic, write \(\log(x) = \log(x) \cdot 1\text{,}\) and hence take \(u(x) \defn \log(x)\) and \(v(x) \defn x\text{.}\)
    2. Since \(\du{u} = \log^\prime(x) \du{x} = \frac{1}{x}\du{x}\text{,}\) compute
      \begin{align*} \int \log(x)\du{x} \amp = \int u \du{v}\\ \amp = uv - \int v \du{u} \\ \amp = \log(x)x - \int x \cdot \frac{1}{x}\du{x}\\ \amp = x\log(x) - \int 1 \du{x} \\ \amp = x\log(x) - x + C \text{.} \end{align*}

    Example 2.2.8.

    Let’s compute \(\int_0^1 \arctan(x) \du{x}\text{.}\)
    1. Since \(\arctan\) is Inverse trigonometric, write \(\arctan(x) = \arctan(x) \cdot 1\text{,}\) and hence take \(u(x) \defn \arctan(x)\) and \(v(x) \defn x\text{.}\)
    2. Since \(\du{u} = \arctan^\prime(x) \du{x} = \frac{1}{1+x^2}\du{x}\text{,}\) compute
      \begin{align*} \int \arctan(x)\du{x} \amp = \int u \du{v}\\ \amp = uv - \int v \du{u} \\ \amp = \arctan(x)x - \int x \cdot \frac{1}{1+x^2}\du{x}\\ \amp = x\arctan(x) - \int \frac{x}{1+x^2} \du{x} \text{.} \end{align*}
      We now use integration by substitution to compute \(\int \frac{x}{1+x^2} \du{x}\text{.}\)
      1. Take \(w \defn 1+x^2\text{,}\) so that \(\du{w} = 2x\du{x}\text{,}\) and hence
        \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{1+x^2} \du{x} = \frac{1}{1+x^2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2x\du{x} = \frac{1}{w} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \du{w} = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{w}\du{w}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      2. Observe that \(\int \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{w}\du{w} = \frac{1}{2}\int \frac{1}{w} \du{w} = \frac{1}{2}\log\abs{w}+C\text{.}\)
      3. Substituting \(w = 1+x^2\) into \(\frac{1}{2}\log\abs{w}\) yields
        \begin{equation*} \int \frac{x}{1+x^2} \du{x} = \frac{1}{2}\log(1+x^2) + C\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Hence, at last,
      \begin{equation*} \int \arctan(x)\du{x} = x\arctan(x) - \frac{1}{2}\log(1+x^2) + C\text{.} \end{equation*}
    3. Finally,
      \begin{align*} \int_0^1 \arctan(x) \du{x} \amp = \eval{x\arctan(x) - \frac{1}{2}\log(1+x^2)}{0}{1} \\ \amp = \left(1\arctan(1) - \frac{1}{2}\log(1+1^2)\right) - \left(0\arctan(0) - \frac{1}{2}\log(1+0^2)\right)\\ \amp = \frac{\pi}{4} - \frac{1}{2}\log(2)\text{.} \end{align*}
    Sometimes, you need to apply integration by parts repeatedly to obtain a non-trivial equation for your unknown integral.

    Example 2.2.9.

    Let’s compute \(\int \eu^x \sin(x) \du{x}\text{.}\)
    1. Since \(\sin(x)\) is Trigonometric and \(\eu^x\) is Exponential, take \(u(x) \defn \sin(x)\) and \(\du{v} \defn \eu^x\du{x}\text{.}\)
    2. Since \(\int\du{v} = \int\eu^x\du{x} = \eu^x + C\text{,}\) take \(v(x) \defn \eu^x\text{.}\)
    3. Since \(\du{u} = \sin^\prime(x) \du{x} = \cos(x)\du{x}\text{,}\) compute
      \begin{align*} \int \eu^x\sin(x)\du{x} \amp = \int u \du{v}\\ \amp = uv - \int v \du{u} \\ \amp = \sin(x)\eu^x - \int \eu^x \cos(x)\du{x}\text{.} \end{align*}
      We now apply integration by parts to compute \(\int \eu^x \cos(x) \du{x}\text{:}\)
      1. Since \(\cos(x)\) is Trigonometric and \(\eu^x\) is Exponential, take \(u(x) \defn \cos(x)\) and \(\du{v} \defn \eu^x\du{x}\text{.}\)
      2. Since \(\int\du{v} = \int\eu^x\du{x} = \eu^x + C\text{,}\) take \(v(x) \defn \eu^x\text{.}\)
      3. Since \(\du{u} = \cos^\prime(x) \du{x} = -\sin(x)\du{x}\text{,}\) compute
        \begin{align*} \int \eu^x\cos(x)\du{x} \amp = \int u \du{v}\\ \amp = uv - \int v \du{u} \\ \amp = \cos(x)\eu^x - \int \eu^x (-\sin(x))\du{x}\\ \amp = \eu^x\cos(x) + \int \eu^x \sin(x) \du{x} \text{.} \end{align*}
      Setting \(I \defn \int \eu^x \sin(x) \du{x}\text{,}\) we therefore find that
      \begin{equation*} I = \eu^x\sin(x) - \left(\eu^x\cos(x)-I\right) = \eu^x\left(\sin(x)-\cos(x)\right) - I\text{,} \end{equation*}
      which we can now solve for \(I\) (with the usual caveats about equalities of indefinite integrals) to get
      \begin{equation*} \int \eu^x \sin(x) \du{x} = I = \frac{\left(\eu^x\cos(x)-I\right) = \eu^x\left(\sin(x)-\cos(x)\right)}{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
