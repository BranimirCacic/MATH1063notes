    Section 2.1 Integration by Substitution

    By the second fundamental theorem of calculus (Theorem 1.7.3), every fact about derivatives should (at least in principle) yield a corresponding fact about integrals. In this section, we turn the chain rule on its head to yield the integration technique called integration by substitution. You should view it as philosophically akin to a familiar strategy from physics: the right change of coordinate system can dramatically simplify an otherwise intractable problem. Our account is complementary to Section 6.1

    Definition 2.1.1.

    Let \(f\) be a function on \([a,b]\) and \(g\) a function on \([c,d]\text{.}\) We say that \(g\) and \(f\) are composable if \(c \leq f(x) \leq d\) for all \(a \leq x \leq b\text{;}\) in this case, their composition is the function \(g \circ f\) on \([a,b]\) defined by \(g \circ f(x) \defn g(f(x))\text{.}\)

    Theorem 2.1.2. Chain rule.

    Let \(f\) be a differentiable function on \([a,b]\) and \(g\) a differentiable function on \([c,d]\text{,}\) and suppose that \(g\) and \(f\) are composable. Then \(g \circ f\) is differentiable on \([a,b]\) with derivative \((g \circ f)^\prime = (g^\prime \circ f) \cdot f^\prime\text{,}\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} (g \circ f)^\prime(x) = g^\prime(f(x)) f^\prime(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 2.1.3.

    Define \(f\) on \([2,4]\) by \(f(x) \defn \log(x)\) and \(g\) on \([1/4,3]\) by \(g(x) \defn \sqrt{x}\text{;}\) note that \(f\) and \(g\) are both differentiable. Let’s show that \(g\) and \(f\) are composable and compute \((g \circ f)^\prime\text{.}\)
    Let’s first check composability. Given \(x \geq 1\text{,}\) note that \(\frac{1}{x} \leq \frac{1}{t} \leq 1\) for all \(1 \leq t \leq x\text{,}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x}(x-1) \leq \int_1^x \frac{1}{t}\du{t} \leq 1(x-1) = x-1 \end{equation*}
    by Lemma 1.6.3. Hence, given \(2 \leq x \leq \text{,}\) it follows that \(g(x) = \log(x) = \int_1^x \frac{1}{t}\du{t}\) satisfies
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{4}(2-1) \leq \frac{1}{x}(x-1) \leq g(x) \leq x-1 \leq 4-1 = 3\text{,} \end{equation*}
    as required. Thus, the composition \(g \circ f\) is well-defined and takes the form
    \begin{equation*} g \circ f(x) = g(f(x)) = \sqrt{f(x)} = \sqrt{\log(x)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Let’s now apply the chain rule to compute \((g \circ f)^\prime\text{.}\) Indeed, since \(f^\prime(x) = \frac{1}{x}\) and \(g^\prime(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\text{,}\) it follows that
    \begin{align*} (g \circ f)^\prime(x) \amp = g^\prime(f(x)) \cdot f^\prime(x)\\ \amp = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{f(x)}} f^\prime(x)\\ \amp = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\log(x)}} \frac{1}{x}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{2x\sqrt{\log(x)}}\text{.} \end{align*}

    Corollary 2.1.4. Integration by substitution.

    Let \(f\) be a continuously differentiable function on \([a,b]\text{,}\) let \(g\) be a continuous function on \([c,d]\text{,}\) and suppose that \(g\) and \(f\) are composable. Then
    \begin{equation*} \int_{f(a)}^{f(b)} g = \int_a^b (g \circ f) \cdot f^\prime\text{,} \end{equation*}
    i.e., in Leibniz notation,
    \begin{equation*} \int_{f(a)}^{f(b)} g(u)\du{u} = \int_a^b g(f(x)) f^\prime(x) \du{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    First, by Theorem 1.6.4, let \(G\) be an antiderivative of \(g\) on \([c,d]\text{.}\) Then, by Theorem 1.7.3,
    \begin{equation*} \int_{f(a)}^{f(b)} = G(f(b)) - G(f(a))\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Next, by Theorem 2.1.2,
    \begin{equation*} (G \circ f)^\prime = (G^\prime \circ f) \cdot f^\prime = (g \circ f) \cdot f^\prime\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so that \(G \circ f\) is an antiderivative of \((g \circ f) \cdot f^\prime\text{.}\) Hence, by Theorem 1.7.3,
    \begin{equation*} \int_a^b (g \circ f) \cdot f^\prime = G\circ f(b) - G \circ f(a) = G(f(b)) - G(f(a))\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus, at last, we find that
    \begin{equation*} \int_{f(a)}^{f(b)} = G(f(b)) - G(f(a)) = \int_a^b (g \circ f) \cdot f^\prime\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Note 2.1.5.

    Recall that \(\int_a^a f \defn 0\) for any function \(f\) defined at \(a\text{.}\) If \(f\) is integrable on \([a,b]\text{,}\) it’s also convenient to define \(\int_b^a f \defn -\int_a^b f\text{.}\) Both fundamental theorems of calculus and the identity \(\int_a^b f = \int_a^c f + \int_c^b f\) can straightforwardly be generalized accordingly.

    Example 2.1.6.

    Let’s use Corollary 2.1.4 to compute \(\int_a^b \sin^5(x) \cos(x)\du{x}\text{.}\) Note that \(\sin^5(x) \cos(x) = \sin(x)^5 \sin^\prime(x)\text{.}\) Hence, let \(f(u) \defn u^5\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{align*} \int_a^b \sin^5(x)\cos(x)\du{x} \amp \int_a^b f(\sin(x)) \sin^\prime(x)\du{x}\\ \amp = \int_{\sin(a)}^{\sin(b)} f(u) \du{u} \\ \amp = \int_{\sin(a)}^{\sin(b)} u^5 \du{u} \\ \amp = \eval{\frac{1}{6}u^6}{\sin(a)}{\sin(b)} \\ \amp = \frac{\sin^6(b) - \sin^6(a)}{6}\text{.} \end{align*}

    Example 2.1.7.

    Let’s compute \(\int_a^b \tan(x)\du{x}\text{,}\) where \([a,b]\) is an interval satisfying \(-\frac{\pi}{2} \lt a \lt b \lt \frac{\pi}{2}\text{;}\) note that \(\cos(x) \gt 0\) on such an interval. Observe that
    \begin{equation*} \tan(x) = \frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)} = \frac{1}{\cos(x)}\sin(x) = -\frac{1}{\cos(x)}\cos^\prime(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Hence, let \(f(u) \defn \frac{1}{u}\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{align*} \int_a^b \tan(x) \du{x} \amp = -\int_a^b f(\cos(x)) \cos^\prime(x) \du{x} \\ \amp =-\int_{\cos(a)}^{\cos(b)} f(u) \du{u}\\ \amp = -\int_{\cos(a)}^{\cos(b)} \frac{1}{u} \du{u}\\ \amp = -\eval{\log(u)}{\cos(a)}{\cos(b)} \\ \amp = \log(\cos(a))-\log(\cos(b))\text{.} \end{align*}

    Heuristic 2.1.8. Integration by substitution in practice.

    To compute \(\int_a^b h(x) \du{x}\) using integration by substitution:
    1. Choose a substitution \(u = g(x)\text{,}\) so that \(\du{u} = g^\prime(x)\du{x}\) (formally!), and reverse engineer \(f\) such that \(h(x)\du{x} = f(u)\du{u}\text{.}\)
    2. Find an antiderivative \(F\) of \(f\text{,}\) i.e., compute \(\int f(u)\du = F(u) + C\text{.}\)
    3. Substitute \(u = g(x)\) into \(F\) to get an antiderivative \(F \circ g\) for \(h\text{.}\)
    4. Apply the second fundamental theorem of calculus (Theorem 1.7.3).
    Moreover, note that the first three steps yield a method for computing the indefinite integral \(\int h(x) \du{x}\text{.}\)

    Example 2.1.9.

    Let’s compute \(\int_a^b \frac{1}{x\log(x)}\du{x}\) for \(a,b \gt 1\text{.}\)
    1. Try \(u = \log(x)\text{.}\) Then \(\du{x} = \log^\prime(x)\du{x} = \frac{1}{x}\du{x}\text{,}\) and hence
      \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x\log(x)}\du{x} = \frac{1}{\log(x)} \cdot \frac{1}{x}\du{x} = \frac{1}{u} \du{u}. \end{equation*}
    2. Recall that \(\int \frac{1}{u} \du{u} = \log(u) + C\text{.}\)
    3. Substituting \(u = \log(x)\) into \(\log(u)\) yields
      \begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{x\log x}\du{x} = \log(\log(x)) + C\text{.} \end{equation*}
    4. At last, by the second fundamental theorem of calculus,
      \begin{equation*} \int_a^b \frac{1}{x\log x}\du{x} = \eval{\log(\log(x))}{a}{b} = \log(\log(b)) - \log(\log(a))\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 2.1.10.

    Let’s find the indefinite integral \(\int \frac{1+\eu^x}{1-\eu^x}\du{x}\text{.}\)
    1. Take \(u = \eu^x\text{,}\) so that \(\du{u} = \eu^x \du{x}\text{,}\) and hence
      \begin{equation*} \frac{1+\eu^x}{1-\eu^x}\du{x} = \frac{1+\eu^x}{1-\eu^x} \cdot \frac{1}{\eu^x} \cdot \eu^x\du{x} = \frac{1+u}{1-u} \cdot \frac{1}{u} \cdot \du{u}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    2. Note that
      \begin{equation*} \frac{1+u}{1-u} \cdot \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1-u+2u}{(1-u)u} = \frac{1}{u} + \frac{2}{1-u}\text{,} \end{equation*}
      so that
      \begin{equation*} \int \frac{1+u}{1-u} \cdot \frac{1}{u} \du{u} = \int \frac{1}{u} \du{u} + 2 \int \frac{1}{1-u}\du{u} = \log\abs{u} + 2\int\frac{1}{1-u}\du{u}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      We compute the remaining term by a substitution of its own:
      1. take \(v = 1-u\text{,}\) so that \(\du{v} = -\du{u}\text{,}\) and hence \(\frac{1}{1-u}\du{u} = -\frac{1}{v}\du{v} \text{;}\)
      2. note that \(-\int\frac{1}{v}\du{v} = -\log\abs{v} + C\text{;}\)
      3. re-substitute \(v = 1-u\) to get \(\int\frac{1}{1-u}\du{u} = -\log\abs{1-u} + C\text{.}\)
      Hence, at last, we get
      \begin{equation*} \int \frac{1+u}{1-u} \cdot \frac{1}{u} \du{u} = \log\abs{u} - 2\log\abs{1-u} + C\text{.} \end{equation*}
    3. We finally re-substitute \(u = \eu^x\) to get
      \begin{equation*} \int \frac{1+\eu^x}{1-\eu^x}\du{x} = \log\abs{\eu^x} - 2\log\abs{1-\eu^x} + C = x - 2\log\abs{1-\eu^x} + C\text{.} \end{equation*}
